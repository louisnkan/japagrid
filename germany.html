Germany

For skilled professionals with formal qualifications who value stability over speed. Germany actively recruits foreign talent, but the path is bureaucratic, language-dependent, and increasingly competitive for housing. I know this because I'm walking it myself.

Verified Last checked 21 July 2026 12 official sources
Visa Pathways

How to get in

Germany has four main routes for non-EU workers. The Skilled Worker Visa is the most direct for Nigerian graduates with recognized qualifications. The Opportunity Card is newer and more flexible but requires points. Choose based on what you have, not what you wish you had.

Most direct

Skilled Worker Visa

For people with a recognized vocational or university qualification and a concrete job offer. This is the path I'm preparing for.

Processing 6–12 weeks
Job first? Yes
Language B1 German
Salary floor €43,800 (2026)

Job Seeker Visa

Six months to find a job. No work allowed during the search. You need savings, a recognized degree, and a tolerance for uncertainty.

Duration 6 months
Blocked account ~€11,000
Degree Recognized required

Opportunity Card (Chancenkarte)

Points-based. No job offer needed. Part-time work allowed. Launched June 2024 — still new, still being figured out by applicants and officials alike.

Duration 1 year, extendable
Part-time work Up to 20 hrs/week
Points needed 6+

Student Visa → Work

Study first, work later. Longer path but you build language, network, and familiarity before you need to earn. This is my backup plan.

Blocked account ~€11,208/year
Work during study 120 full days/year
Job search post-grad 18 months
Job Market

Who Germany actually needs

Germany has a labor shortage. But "shortage" does not mean "easy for everyone." Language requirements are real. Employers prefer candidates already in Germany. And the shortage is sector-specific, not universal.

Software Engineering / IT Very High Demand
Healthcare (Nursing, Caregiving) Very High Demand
Engineering (Mechanical, Electrical) High Demand
Skilled Trades High Demand
Data Science / AI High Demand
Humanities / Social Sciences Low Demand

Reality check: English-speaking jobs exist in tech, but most roles require B1–B2 German. The "Germany needs workers" headline is true for specific sectors. If you're in humanities, this is not your easy path.

Qualifications

Will your degree count?

This is where most Nigerians get stuck. Your degree is not automatically valid. You must get it recognized, and the process varies by profession and federal state. Start this before you apply for jobs.

University Degrees

Check the anabin database. Your Nigerian university and degree need an "H+" rating. If it's "H−", you may need additional coursework.

Check at anabin.kmk.org
Cost €100–€300
Time 2–6 months

Vocational / Trade Qualifications

Handled by state chambers of commerce (IHK/HWK). Recognition is patchy. Some trades have streamlined processes; others require retraining in Germany.

Apply to State IHK/HWK
Cost €100–€600
Time 3–12 months

Critical: Many employers won't consider you without recognition. The "Make it in Germany" portal has a free pre-check tool. Use it.

Living There

What integration actually means

Germany is not a melting pot. Integration is formalized — language courses, civic tests, bureaucracy. The system works if you engage with it. Passive English-speaking expat life is limited.

Healthcare Excellent
Public transport Reliable
Nigerian community Growing (Berlin, Hamburg)
Social acceptance Varies by region
Weather Cold, grey winters
Bureaucracy Extensive
The Truth
Read This

What no one puts in the headline

  • Housing crisis is real. In major cities, finding an apartment takes months. Landlords demand SCHUFA (credit score you don't have), high deposits, and German-speaking references. Start searching before you arrive.
  • Taxes are high. That €55k salary becomes ~€32k net. "High salary" in Germany is not what it sounds like pre-tax.
  • Bureaucracy is a lifestyle. Registration, tax ID, health insurance, bank account — each requires appointments, documents, and patience. The system works, but it moves at its own pace.
  • Language is non-negotiable. You can survive in English in tech. You cannot thrive. B1 is the floor, not the ceiling. Start learning before you arrive.
  • Degree recognition is a gatekeeper. Many Nigerian engineering degrees are recognized, but the process is slow and state-dependent. Don't assume your qualification transfers cleanly.
